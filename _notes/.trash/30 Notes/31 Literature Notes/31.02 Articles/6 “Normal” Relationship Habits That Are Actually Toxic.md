## Highlights
- I believe most of these habits come from movies, books, and TV shows that portray love as this all-or-nothing, all-consuming thing. Dream relationships are never depicted as stable or fulfilling; instead, they’re depicted as dramatic, rocky, and overemotional.
- In most movies, the ideal couple always goes through fights, jealousy scenes, and even cheating. They break up and they get back together because one of them finally has an epiphany and makes every possible effort to reunite.
- Real, strong, true love is not about instability and grand gestures. It’s about sharing our life with someone who adores us and respects us for who we truly are. It’s about mutual effort, acceptance, and understanding.
- Of course, there are challenges, but they’re dealt with in a relatively calm, secure way. There’s no drama involved. In fact, drama should be a warning sign, not something to be proud of.
- Friends tell me this all the time and it’s so damn frustrating. I can’t even count how many girls I know have stayed with the same guy for years, because they have this idea that fighting all the time is a sign of love.
- Conflict is unavoidable but constant, intense conflict is not a sign of passion or love. In fact, it’s a sign of deep-rooted issues like insecurity, immaturity, emotional abuse, or lack of respect — or even lack of love in the first place.
- If you’re unable to say how you feel and why you’re angry, sad, or hurt, you’re only going to become even more frustrated with yourself and your partner.
- Bottling things up and avoiding expressing ourselves is exactly what leads to anger and resentment in a relationship. Speak up instead of holding everything inside you.
- If your partner really loves you, there’s nothing you could share that would make them stop caring about you.
- “This is why so many men and women go from marriages into affairs, then right into a relationship with the affair person. Their spouse can’t fill the hole, so they look to someone else to fill it. That person fills it for awhile and makes them believe it’s not them; it was the person they were with. Until that relationship starts to show its own wear and tear — and ultimately falls apart. And once again, they are left alone with themselves. And the hole.”
- For some reason I don’t understand, our society believes it’s normal to check your partner’s phone, email, or social media.
  No, this is not normal. You’re invading their privacy. Just because someone loves you does not mean their independence and privacy shouldn’t be respected.
  Healthy relationships require absolute trust. If you feel the need to do this, you should either work on your insecurities and self-worth or reconsider your relationship (or both).
- It’s normal for couples to tease each other, but there’s a difference between teasing and making jokes that hurt your partner.
  Teasing can be a way of building intimacy, but it can also cross into disrespect. Someone might be attempting to be playful, but it turns out the other person thinks they’re being hurtful. Or the other way around.
  It all depends on the tone of voice, the body language, and, of course, the intention. One thing is certain: if you feel hurt, you should speak up and let your partner know.
